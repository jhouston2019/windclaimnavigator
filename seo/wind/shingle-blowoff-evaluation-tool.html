Understanding Shingle Wind Damage

Shingle blow-off and uplift are among the most common types of wind damage to residential roofs. Understanding the difference between these damage types and properly documenting them is essential for successful insurance claims. Insurance companies scrutinize shingle damage claims carefully, often arguing that damage is from age, improper installation, or lack of maintenance rather than wind.

Wind Claim Navigator provides specialized evaluation tools that help you assess shingle damage using the same criteria professional adjusters and roofing contractors use, ensuring your documentation meets insurance company standards.

Types of Shingle Wind Damage

  • Complete blow-off: Entire shingles removed from the roof by wind force, exposing underlayment or decking
  • Partial blow-off: Portions of shingles torn away, leaving remaining material attached
  • Shingle uplift: Shingles lifted and creased but not completely removed, compromising seal and weather resistance
  • Tab tearing: Individual shingle tabs torn or separated from the main body
  • Seal failure: Wind breaks the adhesive seal between shingle layers, allowing future blow-off
  • Granule loss: Excessive granule removal from wind abrasion or impact
  • Edge lifting: Shingle edges lifted along rakes, eaves, or ridges
  • Cracking and splitting: Shingles cracked or split from wind stress

Why Shingle Damage Claims Get Challenged

Insurance companies frequently dispute shingle damage claims by arguing:

  • Age-related failure: Claiming shingles failed due to age rather than wind
  • Improper installation: Suggesting inadequate nailing or sealing caused failure
  • Manufacturing defect: Arguing damage is from product defects, not wind
  • Thermal damage: Claiming damage is from heat cycling rather than wind
  • Insufficient wind speeds: Asserting wind speeds weren't high enough to cause damage
  • Pre-existing condition: Claiming damage existed before the wind event

How to Evaluate Shingle Wind Damage

Proper evaluation of shingle wind damage requires systematic assessment and detailed documentation. Wind Claim Navigator guides you through this process:

Step 1: Safety Assessment

  • Never climb on a damaged roof without proper safety equipment
  • Use binoculars, zoom cameras, or drones for elevated inspection
  • If safe access is available, inspect from roof edges only
  • Consider professional inspection for steep or high roofs

Step 2: Pattern Recognition

Wind damage typically shows specific patterns that distinguish it from other types of damage:

  • Directional patterns: Damage concentrated on windward-facing slopes
  • Edge concentration: More damage along roof edges, rakes, and ridges where wind forces are highest
  • Corner emphasis: Increased damage at roof corners where wind creates uplift
  • Random distribution: Scattered damage across the roof field from turbulent winds
  • Consistent damage type: Similar damage mechanisms across affected areas

Step 3: Damage Quantification

Document the extent of shingle damage:

  • Count the number of missing or damaged shingles
  • Measure the total square footage of affected areas
  • Note the percentage of roof area damaged
  • Document damage to multiple roof slopes or sections
  • Identify damage to ridge caps, starter strips, and edge shingles

Step 4: Photographic Documentation

Comprehensive photo documentation is essential:

  • Photograph the entire roof from ground level (all four sides)
  • Take close-up photos of specific damage areas
  • Capture images showing damage patterns and distribution
  • Photograph exposed underlayment or decking
  • Document any fallen shingles on the ground
  • Take photos from inside the attic showing roof penetration
  • Include reference objects for scale in photos

Key Evidence for Shingle Wind Damage Claims

To successfully claim shingle wind damage, you need evidence that proves wind caused the damage:

Weather Documentation

  • National Weather Service reports showing wind speeds in your area
  • Local weather station data for the date and time of the wind event
  • Storm reports documenting high winds in your region
  • Photographs or videos of the wind event if available

Physical Evidence

  • Fallen shingles collected from your property
  • Photos showing nail holes in blown-off shingles (proving proper attachment)
  • Documentation of damage patterns consistent with wind
  • Evidence of similar damage to neighboring properties

Professional Assessment

  • Contractor inspection reports attributing damage to wind
  • Engineering evaluations for severe or disputed damage
  • Repair estimates from licensed roofing contractors
  • Expert opinions on wind speeds required to cause observed damage
