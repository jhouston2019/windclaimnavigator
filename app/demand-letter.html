What a Demand Letter Does

A well-structured demand letter anchors your position, documents valuation, and starts a response clock. Insurers respond because it memorializes facts, cites duties, and signals your readiness to escalate if ignored.

Send it after you have clear loss valuation and evidence assembled, especially when timelines slip or offers are inadequate. Include every element that proves damages and highlights carrier delays.

Core Elements to Include

  • Summary of loss
  • Policy references
  • Supporting evidence list
  • Valuation amounts
  • Carrier delays or violations
  • Requested action
  • Deadline for response

Avoid weak language, missing proof, or open-ended timelines—those mistakes weaken leverage and slow fair settlement.

Demand Structure & Framework

Use this outline to keep the letter concise, evidence-backed, and deadline-driven.

Required Sections

Follow this order so the carrier cannot claim confusion about the facts, numbers, or requested action.

  • Policyholder introduction
  • Statement of loss summary
  • Evidence list with exhibits
  • Breakdown of damages (RCV/ACV)
  • Carrier delays or violations
  • Statutory references (generic)
  • Demand for payment and interest
  • Deadline to respond
  • Reservation of rights

Demand Letter Framework

Introduction

Identify policyholder, property, policy number, and claim.

Loss Narrative

Summarize date, cause, affected areas, and mitigation actions.

Evidence Overview

Reference photos, estimates, statements, receipts, and logs.

Valuation Summary

Present RCV/ACV, contents, ALE/LOU, and totals with exhibits.

Carrier Conduct

Note delays, inadequate responses, or unmet requests.

Demand for Action

Request payment, interest, and corrective steps.

Deadline

Set a clear timeframe (10–14 days) for response.

Closing & Signature

State reservation of rights and preferred contact.

Deadlines Create Pressure

Insurers must acknowledge, investigate, and respond within statutory or policy timelines. A stated deadline backed by evidence forces activity and reduces open-ended delays.

  • Reference typical response windows (acknowledgement, investigation, payment).
  • Give a firm timeframe to answer your demand and provide payment status.
  • State that unmet deadlines may be escalated to the Department of Insurance.
  • Keep proof of delivery to show the clock started.

Recommended Deadline

10–14 Days

Short enough to create urgency, long enough to be reasonable. Reiterate that silence triggers escalation.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Prevent slowdowns and weak leverage by eliminating these gaps before you send the letter.

  • Lack of valuation amounts
  • Emotional or aggressive tone
  • Missing policy references
  • No evidence attached
  • No deadline for response
  • Failing to cite carrier delays
  • Not documenting prior requests
  • Vague or incomplete demands
