State Complaint Processes

How to file with your state regulator, what to include, and how complaints pressure timely, fair claim handling.

Authority Guides › State Complaint Processes

On This Page

    Overview

    Every state insurance department accepts consumer complaints about delayed payments, unfair denials, or poor communication. Filing triggers a regulatory inquiry that often accelerates responses and forces carriers to document their position.

    Key Rules & Requirements

    • Eligibility: Policyholders, authorized representatives, and sometimes contractors can file.
    • Scope: Departments review compliance with statutes and policy terms; they cannot award damages but can compel responses and levy penalties.
    • Timelines: Many states require carriers to respond to the department within 10–20 days.
    • Documentation: Complaints should include policy info, timelines, prior requests, and disputed amounts.
    • No retaliation: States prohibit carriers from penalizing consumers for filing.

    Step-by-Step Guidance

    1. Download or open your state’s online complaint form from the insurance department site.
    2. Provide policy number, claim number, and carrier contacts.
    3. Describe the issue concisely: dates of loss, notice, inspections, payments, and the specific delay or denial.
    4. Attach supporting documents (denial letters, estimates, emails showing missed deadlines).
    5. State the desired resolution: payment of undisputed amount, written decision, supervisor review, or statutory interest.
    6. Submit and save the confirmation number. Calendar the department’s expected response window.
    7. When the carrier replies, review for completeness and follow up with the department if issues remain.

    Required Documentation

    • Policy declarations page and claim number.
    • Timeline of communications and missed deadlines.
    • Carrier estimates, denial letters, or reservation of rights letters.
    • Independent estimates, photos, or expert reports showing disputed scope/valuation.
    • Any prior written complaints or escalation emails.

    Common Insurer Tactics

    • Providing minimal responses to regulators that omit context or documents.
    • Claiming the matter is “under investigation” without specific outstanding items.
    • Blaming third-party vendors for delay (engineers, contractors) to avoid accountability.
    • Offering small nuisance payments to close the complaint without addressing the issue.

    Red Flags to Watch For

    • No carrier response to the department within the required timeframe.
    • Responses that do not attach the policy provisions relied on.
    • Contradictions between what the adjuster told you and what is reported to the regulator.
    • Refusal to release claim file notes after the complaint is resolved.

    Best Practices for Policyholders

    • Keep the narrative short, factual, and tied to statutes or policy clauses.
    • Ask for a copy of the carrier’s full response to the department.
    • Escalate internally before filing, but don’t wait so long that deadlines expire.
    • After resolution, request written confirmation of any commitments made.
    • If issues persist, consider counsel and note any bad-faith indicators raised.
